Flaky Network: Reliable Real-Time Streaming over UDP

Architecture

The system consists of a sender and receiver communicating through a hostile UDP relay. The harness source provides one 160-byte frame every 20 ms to the sender. The sender adds protocol metadata and proactive redundancy before transmitting packets through the relay. The receiver processes arriving packets and delivers recovered frames to the harness player.

Sender Design

Each original frame is transmitted as a data packet containing a packet type, sequence number, and the original 160-byte payload. Frames are grouped into pairs. For every two data frames, the sender calculates the bitwise XOR of their payloads and transmits an additional parity packet.

Receiver Design

The receiver identifies packets by type and sequence number. Data packets are stored in an indexed frame buffer, allowing reordered packets to be processed correctly and duplicate packets to be ignored. Received data frames are forwarded to the player.

Loss Recovery

For each two-frame FEC group, the receiver stores the XOR parity packet. If one of the two data frames is missing while the other frame and parity packet are available, the missing payload is reconstructed by XORing the available payload with the parity payload. The reconstructed frame is then sent to the player.

Design Choices

Forward Error Correction was selected instead of retransmission because retransmission requires feedback and another traversal of the hostile network, which increases recovery latency. Proactive parity allows recovery immediately when sufficient packets reach the receiver.

Performance

The initial naive implementation suffered substantial deadline misses. A four-data-frame XOR group improved reliability but remained insufficient under the harsher network profile. Reducing the FEC group to two data frames plus one parity packet achieved a bandwidth overhead of approximately 1.55x. Profile A was successfully validated at a playout delay as low as 50 ms, while the harsher Profile B required a 100 ms playout delay to maintain the deadline-miss rate below the required 1% threshold. Therefore, 100 ms was selected as the recommended grading delay to provide greater robustness across varying and unseen network conditions.

Limitations

The XOR scheme can recover only one missing data frame per FEC group. Severe burst losses may remove multiple required packets and prevent recovery. Packets arriving after the playout deadline also cannot prevent a deadline miss.
